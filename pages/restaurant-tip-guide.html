Restaurant Tip Guide 2026 — How Much Should You Tip?

The standard tip at a sit-down restaurant in the United States is 18-20% of the pre-tax bill. This has been the norm for several years and remains the expectation in 2026. Tipping customs vary by restaurant type, service style, and situation. This guide covers the most common scenarios so you always know the right amount to leave.

When Should You Tip More Than 20%?

There are situations where tipping above the standard 20% is appropriate and appreciated:

  • Large groups: Tables of 6 or more often have an auto-gratuity of 18-20%. If not, tip 20% or more since large tables require more server attention.
  • Exceptional service: If a server went above and beyond — accommodating special requests, managing dietary restrictions carefully, or providing outstanding recommendations — 25% shows genuine appreciation.
  • Holidays: Servers working on holidays like Thanksgiving, Christmas Eve, or New Year's are sacrificing time with their families. An extra 5-10% above your usual tip is a kind gesture.
  • Complex orders: Multiple modifications, split checks for a large party, or complicated dietary requirements all increase server workload.

Should You Tip on Pre-Tax or Post-Tax Amount?

Etiquette experts generally agree: tip on the pre-tax subtotal. Sales tax varies by state and city — it is not part of the service you received. That said, many people tip on the total bill including tax, which is fine. The difference is usually small. On a $50 bill with 8% tax, the difference between tipping 20% pre-tax ($10.00) and 20% post-tax ($10.80) is just 80 cents.

How Do You Calculate a Tip Quickly in Your Head?

If you do not have freetipcalculator.app handy, here are mental math shortcuts:

  • 20% tip: Move the decimal point one place left (10%), then double it. A $45.00 bill becomes $4.50, doubled to $9.00.
  • 15% tip: Calculate 10% (move decimal), then add half of that. $45.00 becomes $4.50 + $2.25 = $6.75.
  • 18% tip: Calculate 20% and subtract a small amount. Or calculate 10% + 10% and subtract 2% (one-fifth of the 10%).
